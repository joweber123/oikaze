キュウシュウ

In a nutshell

Kyushu reminds one that it is ancient without saying a word. Staring at the mud bubbling out from deep in the Earth you feel the ripples of Japan’s primordial past. Volcanic eruptions continue to spread their vibrations throughout the terrain here. The Japanese that live here have come to embrace this fierce region and relish in the natural hot springs that gush from deep below the earth’s crust. The Japanese tradition of conserving natural features while honoring them with shrines could’ve started at the Onsen Hot Springs in the Mountains of Kyushu. Nature and luxury are brought together to soothe the soul and the body. Kyushu is home to some of Japan’s most famous hot spring towns. Kurokawa, Beppu and Yufuin are recognized nationally and many Japanese take the trip down to this southern island just to soak in these waters. A ferry ride to the south is the island of Yakushima who’s ancient cedar forests are home to some of the oldest trees in the world. The whole island is a national park and for good reason. The forest is so inspiring that the artists who created the film “Princess Mononoke” spent weeks here drawing from the inspiration from under ancient trees. The subtropical climate of this region keep the forests covered in a perpetual mist, drawing you into a seemingly alien world. The coastline rivals Big Sur, but without the traffic… unless you consider monkees and deer traffic. Yakushima also has a sea-side onsen that only appears at low-tide. This is perhaps the most magical Onsen of all - cold sea waves slowly splashing towards your hot bath in granite depressions in the stone shore. Whether you come for the nature, for the history or for the culture, Kyushu does not disappoint.
